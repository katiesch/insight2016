Predicting Graffiti Rates in San Diego

2016/09/24

The mean popularity of explicit tracks is approximately 59, while the clean versions score around 32. Although these distributions aren't strictly Gaussian, they both have a standard deviation of around 12. Just one additional note, I also removed the Deluxe version of 'Watch the Throne,' as I thought it was a bit unfair to compare something deluxe to something standard.

Right, so explicit versions it is then. I get it; if you're listening to Kanye, you want to listen to Kanye being Kanye, and that usually involves curse words. When you look at the difference in popularity between song versions, there's a typical difference of around 25 points.

Note that the largest difference is for 'Ni**as in Paris,' which is peak Kanye being Kanye (although I don't think that's a quanitifable statement). I've also color-coded the songs in the chart above by album, which shows that the difference in popularity between explicit and clean versions is typically smaller for "Late Registration" than for "Watch the Throne" and "College Dropout." I've also overplotted a linear regression (the black dashed line), which can be used to predict the typical offset in popularity between clean and explicit versions based on a song's popularity. It is largely anchored by the three points on the left hand side, as most of the stars are in a clump around a difference of 25.

Now on to the meat of the question: is Kanye better off doing his own thing or working with others?

The distributions of popularity for solo and collaborative tracks are quite similar to one another. In fact, the average popularity for his solo work is 61, while it is 57 for his collaborative.

But are certain collaborations more successful than others? For example, Kanye often teams up with Jay Z; in fact "Watch the Throne" was a whole album by the two of them. But does working together benefit Kanye, popularity-wise?

The figure above looks at the mean popularity of the tracks with a particular collaborator. The red solid line is the mean popularity for Kanye alone, and the red dashed lines show the standard deviation on this value. The blue solid line shows the mean popularity of tracks Kanye makes with collaborators. Jay Z is highlighted in gold, and his tracks are actually less popular than Kanye alone. In fact, 'Watch the Throne' has the lowest popularity rating, although that's likely a function of more people listening to the Deluxe version, which has a popularity of 80. Time to reevaluate that friendship? Eh, they're still well within the standard deviation of Kanye's popularity alone, so I think they're cool. I guess 'Jay is chillin, 'Ye is chillin, What more can I say?'

By this point I'm sure you noticed the elephant in the room. Namely, one collaborator drags down Kanye by more than 2 sigma. Who could that be? Oh right, it's Chris Martin from Coldplay.

Chris Martin is featured on the song 'Homecoming' from the album 'Graduation.' It's a love song from Kanye to Chicago, and has a fantastic video directed by Hype Williams.

'Graduation', circled in cyan above, is slightly above average in popularity as an album. And this song, while near the end of the album, is not the last one. The following track, "Big Brother", has a score of 55. So it's not just that people don't make it all the way through the album.

Chris Martin is fairly different as far as Kanye's collaborators go. Namely, he's not a rapper. So perhaps this is the difference. However, Kanye teamed up with Adam Levine on 'Heard 'Em Say.' Adam Levine and Chris Martin appeal to a similar demographic, which doesn't necessarily include Kanye fans, but Adam's track has a popularity of 62. Additionally, Bon Iver is on the track 'Lost in the World' on 'My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy,' which has a score of 58. He's also on 'Monster,' but that track has so many other rappers on it, that it's popularity of 68 is likely more a factor of Nicki Minaj (who kills it in that song) than him. Coldplay, which is largely the band Chris Martin is associated with, has an overall popularity score of 89, only slightly lower than Kanye's score of 92. So clearly, there is a strong Chris Martin fan base on Spotify.

Kanye himself seems to be pretty happy with the track. In 'Big Brother', he raps about Jay Z copying his good idea:

"I told Jay I did a song with Coldplay
Next thing I know he got a song with Coldplay
Back in my mind I'm like damn, no way"

This begs the question, is Jay Z also negatively affected by having Chris Martin around? I ran a similar analysis on his albums. Below I show individual explicit Jay Z tracks, color-coded by album. The black solid line shows the mean popularity of the tracks, with the dashed lines showing the standard deviation. Chris Martin is featured on the track 'Beach Chair' on 'Kingdom Come.' Jay Z fans don't seem to have the same issue with Chris Martin. While the track is slightly lower than average in popularity, it is nowhere near the extremes we saw in Kanye's discography. Jay Z has his own extreme outlier, the song "The Return" on "Unfinished Business." Note that there are many more Jay Z albums, such as "Vol 2. Hard Knock Life," etc. However, these are not available for US markets, presumably due to his competing streaming service Tidal.

Alright, so now we see while Kanye fans do not enjoy his collaboration with Chris Martin, Jay Z fans are pretty much neutral about his. Perhaps the song "Homecoming" is just not up to par...Except, the Recording Industry Association of America reports that it went Platinum, yet it is significantly less popular on Spotify than tracks that went Gold or did not rate at all.

This implies that Kanye fans on Spotify don't like Chris Martin. Since the song itself did well on the charts, it suggests that the general disdain for "Homecoming" is more a factor of Chris Martin than anything else. There are probably a number of factors related to the unpopularity of this track; unfortunately, I don't have access to Spotify data about listener demographics to learn more. All of that info is proprietary. So I'm going to look at a few more things, namely my original question: is it beneficial to work with Kanye?

I used spotipy to pull out all of the tracks for each one of Kanye's collaborators. I then compared their solo song popularity to the popularity of their tracks with Kanye. Note that I have not trimmed out the Edited versions of these tracks, which leads to the lower overall popularity for each artist. The black dotted line shows a one-to-one relationship, namely that their own stuff is comparably popular to their material with Kanye. For the majority of artists, Kanye's presence has a positive affect on their track popularity, lying above the one-to-one relation. However, you can see that at around a popularity of 38, this changes. The red line shows a linear regression fit to the, admittedly sparse, points. As an artist is more popular on their own, Kanye West has a negative affect on their track popularity. For example, Nicki Minaj and John Legend are much more popular on their own than on their tracks with Kanye.

Thus, while it is not necessarily beneficial for Kanye to collaborate with others on his own tracks, it is generally advantageous to work with Kanye, unless you're already quite a popular musician in your own right. On average, the mean popularity of a track with him is 9 points higher than one without him!
